## Purpose
To build a distinctive space transport and logistics web game that offers simulation depth, systemic integrity, and a satisfying loop of complexity management.

## Good End State
A shippable web-native experience where players engage deeply with logistics systems, feeling the weight of their decisions without being overwhelmed by UI or technical friction.

## Failure Modes
- Feature creep that dilutes the core logistics loop.
- Prioritizing realism over fun, resulting in a spreadsheet simulator.
- Technical debt preventing web performance standards.
- Shipping incomplete systems that frustrate rather than engage.

## Vision
The Game pillar defines the product itself: a space transport and logistics experience designed for the web. This genre is chosen not merely for its thematic appeal but for its structural suitability to incremental development and systemic depth. Logistics games are about flow, optimization, and the satisfaction of solving complex problems. However, many fail by becoming too abstract or too cumbersome. Our distinctive quality must lie in the tactile feel of the transport mechanisms and the clarity of the information presented to the player. It is not enough to move goods from point A to point B; the player must feel the tension of supply chains, the risk of disruption, and the reward of efficiency. This requires a simulation depth that is plausible but not burdensome. We are building a world that behaves consistently, where rules are discoverable and consequences are logical. The web platform imposes specific constraints that must be turned into advantages. Load times must be minimal, state management must be robust against connection drops, and the UI must be responsive across devices. Shippable quality does not mean feature-complete; it means polished within its scope. A small, functioning system is better than a large, broken one.

What makes this game distinctive is the commitment to simulation integrity without sacrificing accessibility. Many logistics games hide complexity behind walls of numbers. We aim to visualize complexity, making the state of the network visible and understandable. This requires careful design of the interface and the feedback loops. When a shipment is delayed, the player should know why and feel the impact. When a route is optimized, the reward should be immediate and clear. This distinctiveness is our competitive edge. However, the risk of feature creep is high. Logistics systems can expand infinitely. We must resist the urge to add every possible mechanic. Instead, we focus on the core thesis: transport and logistics with simulation depth. Every feature must be tested against this thesis. If it does not deepen the logistics experience, it is cut. Technical debt is another critical failure mode. Web games often suffer from performance issues as they grow. We must prioritize architecture that scales, ensuring that the simulation remains performant as the player's network expands. This means investing in efficient data structures and rendering pipelines early.

Shippable quality also implies a respect for the player's time. The game must be saveable, resumable, and fair. Progress should not be lost due to technical failures. The testing regime must be rigorous, covering not just code correctness but gameplay balance. A bug in a logistics game can break the economy, rendering hours of play meaningless. Therefore, validation is crucial. The relationship with the Self pillar ensures that this quality is maintained through disciplined loops. The relationship with the Monetization pillar ensures that the game is designed for value, not just engagement. We are not building a Skinner box; we are building a simulation worth paying for. The Game pillar is the heart of the studio. If the game is not compelling, nothing else matters. It must be distinctive, stable, and deeply satisfying to play.
