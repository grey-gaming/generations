# Game Vision

Version: 1
Refined at loop: 0

## Purpose
To create a distinctive space transport and logistics simulation that offers depth without overwhelming complexity, focusing on the elegance of systems rather than the spectacle of action.

## Good End State
A living universe where players engage deeply with the systems, creating their own stories through their logistics networks, respecting the player's intelligence and time.

## Failure Modes
- Feature creep diluting the focus on the core logistics loop.
- The spreadsheet trap where simulation becomes too abstract and loses the fantasy.
- Technical debt accumulating faster than it can be paid down.
- Releasing unfinished vertical slices that feel like prototypes rather than products.

## Relationships
- Game needs Self to build it efficiently, but Self needs Game to prove its capabilities. If Game development moves too fast for Self's validation pipelines, quality suffers. Game must provide enough value to justify commercial support, but Monetization must not dictate design decisions that compromise the player experience.

## Vision
The Game pillar represents the primary artifact of the studio, the tangible product that delivers value to players and validates the existence of the platform. For Generations, the game is a space transport and logistics simulation designed to offer depth without overwhelming complexity. The purpose of this pillar is to create a distinctive experience that stands out in a crowded market by focusing on the elegance of systems rather than the spectacle of action. The distinctive quality of this game lies in its commitment to simulation integrity; every transport route, every cargo manifest, and every economic fluctuation must feel grounded in a consistent internal logic that rewards player understanding and planning. This is not a game about twitch reflexes or random number generation; it is a game about building networks, optimizing flows, and managing scarcity in a vast, procedurally generated universe. The choice of space as a setting is deliberate; it allows for abstracted physics and large scales that emphasize the logistics over the terrain, giving players the freedom to define their own paths without the constraints of earthly geography. Shippable quality is defined not by the number of features but by the polish and stability of the core loop. A shippable version must be free of critical bugs, possess a coherent user interface, and provide a satisfying experience even in its earliest vertical slice. It must feel finished, not like a prototype waiting for content. Failure modes for the Game pillar are numerous and often seductive. Feature creep is the most common danger, where the desire to add more ships, more planets, or more mechanics dilutes the focus on the core logistics loop. Another failure mode is the spreadsheet trap, where the simulation becomes so abstract that it loses the fantasy of space travel and becomes merely a data entry exercise. There is also the risk of technical debt accumulating faster than it can be paid down, leading to a codebase that is impossible to iterate on without breaking existing systems. The distinctive visual style should reflect the functional nature of the gameplay, using UI and feedback to communicate complex states clearly without clutter. Performance is also a key component of shippable quality; the game must run smoothly on target hardware, handling large-scale simulations without lag or stutter. This requires careful architectural decisions early in development, prioritizing data-oriented design and efficient rendering pipelines. The game must also be designed for longevity, with systems that can expand over time without requiring fundamental rewrites. This means modularizing mechanics and ensuring that new content can be added without disrupting the balance of the existing economy. Ultimately, the Game pillar is the proof of work for the entire studio; if the game is not compelling, nothing else matters. It must be built with patience and precision, prioritizing depth over breadth and quality over speed. The player's mind is the real terrain we are mapping, and the game must respect their cognitive load while offering enough complexity to remain engaging over hundreds of hours. The narrative is emergent, told through the success and failure of supply chains, rather than through scripted cutscenes. This requires a robust simulation engine that can handle unexpected player behaviors without breaking, ensuring that the world feels alive and responsive to the player's actions.
